Research

My research centers on finding quantitative methodologies to map product design variables (design input x) to customers’ preference (design output y) through functional (z) and non-functional (z’) attributes (See Figure 1). If designers understand this mapping, they can better predict customers’ preference at an early stage of design. The challenge is that customers’ preference is based not only on functional product attributes but also on non-functional attributes that are difficult to quantify. For example, functional product attributes such as fuel economy are quantitatively measurable, thus output is readily predictable as long as we know the design variables. On the other hand, due to the subjective traits and heterogeneous customer perceptions of non-functional attributes such as vehicle styling, design variables do not lead to predictable outputs. Moreover, non-functional attributes are not typically determined by engineering designers alone. Instead, these attributes are generally decided by designers from different domains such as industrial design, ergonomics, or operations. Therefore, understanding how their decisions are related is necessary when they share design variables that lead to trade-offs of other designers’ goals.

I have three main research ideas that branched out from my original research motivation. These three research areas are complementary in the sense that they help understand the original big picture question proposed in Figure 1. However, they are also distinct in that each area exists as a discipline of its own.

[1] Design for Market Systems
My main research integrates different design domains into a decision making framework. I have been working on electric vehicle (EV) and autonomous vehicle design problems by integrating electric powertrain simulation, autonomous fleet assignment simulation, charging station location network, consumer demand, and public policy models, into a large decision making framework. I compared the public investment impacts on EV markets in China and US, and extended this framework to autonomous vehicle sharing services.

[2] Design for Perceptual Attributes
Although consumers’ preference model is a core part in Design for Market Systems, capturing preferences on perceptual attributes is still a challenge. I have been examining the trade-off between perceptions of comfort and styling in automotive seats. I elicit consumers’ preferences on seat design using parameterized 3D seat shapes and test the effects of specific attributes, such as bolster height, on the perception of comfort and styling.

[3] Systems Design
One practical issue of Design for Market Systems is that a system problem may not be solvable due to size and complexity of models. Analytical Target Cascading (ATC) method has been developed to address this issue by partitioning a system into subsystems and coordinating them to meet a system goal. I have been designing commercial vehicle systems and hybrid vehicle architectures using ATC.
